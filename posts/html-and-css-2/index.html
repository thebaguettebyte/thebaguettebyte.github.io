Doctor? Nay, a !DOCTYPE

The !DOCTYPE sits proudly at the very top of your HTML document, like a noble herald announcing to the browser, “We shall be speaking in HTML5 today, thank you very much.” Its job? Simply to let the browser know which version of HTML it’s dealing with.

ID’S and Classes

Remember our delightful little chat about tags? Well, these fine fellows can be adorned with ids and classes, much like a monocle and top hat, like so:

<div id="container"></div>
<p class="paragraph-tag">Baguette</p>

You may christen your ids and classes as you please, but do keep them tidy and meaningful. One never knows when one must revisit the code, and deciphering nonsense names is frightfully uncivilized. Now, the distinction:

  • An id is unique, one per page, like a royal title.
  • A class may be used repeatedly, much like a fashionable cravat at high tea.

I’ve noticed there are other subtle differences too, but I shall investigate further and report back in due course.

Attributes, the dainty carry-ons of tags

There exists a veritable smorgasbord of attributes, just as there is a delightful array of tags. An attribute, you see, is the distinguished word that follows directly after the tag — much like a loyal valet. Consider id or class, for instance. Observe the example below for a dash of clarity:

<img src="path-to-img.jpg" alt="A man riding a red bike." />

The img (image) tag comes adorned with a rather important attribute called src, short for source, which tells the browser where your image resides, like a posh forwarding address. Then we have the ever-considerate alt attribute, which stands for alternative text. This gallant little fellow steps in should your image fail to appear, offering a textual description in its stead. Most nobly, it also serves our visually impaired guests, allowing screen readers to proclaim aloud what would otherwise go unseen. A true gentleman’s touch, wouldn’t you say?
